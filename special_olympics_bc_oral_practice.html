Speaker 1 — Introduction and Origin

Suggested time: 1.5–2 minutes
Listen to model audio

Good morning everyone. Today our group is presenting about Special Olympics BC.

Special Olympics BC is part of a larger movement that supports people with intellectual disabilities through sport, friendship, health, and community inclusion. This topic is important because it connects to disability rights, the social model of disability, and inclusion in schools.

Special Olympics did not begin only as a sports competition. It began because people started to challenge unfair beliefs about people with intellectual disabilities.

In the early 1960s, Dr. Frank Hayden, a Canadian researcher at the University of Toronto, studied children with intellectual disabilities at Beverley School in Toronto. At that time, many people believed these children could not be physically active or successful in sports.

Dr. Hayden’s research showed something very important. He found that many children with intellectual disabilities had low physical fitness not because they were unable, but because they had not been given enough chances to play, train, and participate.

This idea helped inspire the Special Olympics movement. Eunice Kennedy Shriver helped organize the first Special Olympics event in Chicago in 1968. Canada was represented at that first event, and later Harry “Red” Foster helped bring Special Olympics to Canada.

The first Canadian Special Olympics event was held in Toronto in 1969.

Special Olympics BC began in 1980. At first, it started with two weekends of games and about 500 participants.

Since then, Special Olympics BC has grown into year-round programs in many communities across British Columbia. It is no longer just one event. It includes regular training, competitions, youth programs, health programs, and community activities.

Athletes can participate in many sports, such as swimming, basketball, bowling, soccer, floor hockey, track and field, and more.

The purpose of Special Olympics BC is not only to win medals. Winning can be exciting, but the bigger purpose is to help athletes build confidence, health, friendships, teamwork, independence, and belonging.

Special Olympics BC also helps the public see people with intellectual disabilities in a more respectful way. Instead of focusing only on what someone cannot do, it focuses on what people can do when they are given support and opportunity.

This connects strongly to inclusion because athletes are not being hidden away or separated from the community. They are participating, competing, leading, and being celebrated.

Now we will connect Special Olympics BC to the social model of disability.

The social model of disability says that disability is not only caused by a person’s impairment or diagnosis. Disability is also created by barriers in society.

These barriers can include low expectations, inaccessible programs, negative attitudes, lack of support, and being excluded from activities.

For example, if a student wants to join a game but the instructions are confusing, the rules are too fast, and nobody adapts the activity, the problem is not only inside the student. The problem is also in the environment.

Special Olympics BC helps remove these barriers. It gives people with intellectual disabilities real opportunities to train, compete, make friends, and be recognized for their strengths.

This contributes to rights because rights are not only about laws. Rights are also about real access, real participation, and real respect.

Special Olympics BC shows that people with intellectual disabilities have the right to participate in community life, to be included in sports, and to be proud of their achievements.

It also changes public attitudes. It helps society move from pity to respect, and from exclusion to inclusion.

Special Olympics BC is also connected to inclusion in schools.

Inclusion does not mean a student is simply placed in the same classroom or gym. Real inclusion means the student participates, learns, makes choices, builds friendships, and feels valued.

Special Olympics BC has youth programs that support children and teenagers. Programs such as Active Start and FUNdamentals help younger children develop movement skills, sport skills, and social skills.

Unified Sports is especially important for schools because it brings students with and without intellectual disabilities together on the same team.

This is a strong example of inclusion. Students are not separated into “helpers” and “people who need help.” Instead, everyone participates together. They learn teamwork, patience, communication, and respect.

In schools, sports can sometimes create exclusion. Some students may be picked last, left out, or asked to watch instead of play. But inclusive sports can change this.

For example, a teacher or EA can adapt a game by slowing the pace, using a bigger ball, giving visual instructions, creating partner roles, or focusing on teamwork instead of only winning.

Special Olympics BC reminds us that inclusion must be active. We need to create real chances for students to join, not just say they are welcome.

As future Education Assistants, we need to know about Special Olympics BC because EAs often support students who face barriers in school.

An EA may support a student in gym class, recess, field trips, clubs, classroom group work, or social activities. The EA might help by breaking instructions into smaller steps, using visuals, modelling a skill, encouraging peer support, or helping the student manage anxiety.

Special Olympics BC reminds us that our job is not to do everything for the student. Our job is to support independence, confidence, participation, and belonging.

Sometimes the most important support is helping classmates understand how to include someone respectfully.

Class Activity: Barrier or Inclusion?

Now we have a short activity for the class. We will read three examples. Please decide if each one shows a barrier or inclusion.

Example one: A student watches gym class from the side because the game is too fast.
Answer: This is a barrier.

Example two: The teacher changes the rules so every student has a role on the team.
Answer: This is inclusion.

Example three: Students with disabilities play only in a separate room and never join classmates.
Answer: This is segregation, not full inclusion.

To close, Special Olympics BC teaches us that people with intellectual disabilities need opportunity, support, and respect. Inclusion is not passive. It is something we create through our choices, our attitudes, and our actions.

Opportunity + Support = Inclusion
