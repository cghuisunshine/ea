First, we can see the idea moving from just present, to participating, and finally to belonging.

Being just present means the student is in the same classroom or gym, but may not really be involved. They may only watch, wait, or feel left out.

The next step is participating. This means the student is doing the activity, making choices, practising skills, and joining with others.

The final goal is belonging. Belonging means the student feels accepted, valued, and part of the group.

Special Olympics BC connects to inclusion in several ways.

First, there are youth programs. Programs like Active Start and FUNdamentals help children develop movement skills, sport skills, social skills, and confidence.

Second, there is Unified Sports. In Unified Sports, students with and without intellectual disabilities train and compete on the same team.

This is very important because it changes “helping” into teamwork. Students are not separated into “helpers” and “students who need help.” Instead, everyone has a role, and everyone participates as teammates.

Third, Special Olympics BC also gives ideas for inclusive gym activities. For example, a teacher or EA can use a bigger ball, slow down the pace, create partner roles, give visual instructions, and focus on teamwork goals.

So, real inclusion is not only about being in the same place. Real inclusion means students participate, build friendships, and feel that they belong.
