Suggested time: about 2 minutes

Good morning everyone. Today our group is presenting about Special Olympics BC.

Special Olympics BC is part of the larger Special Olympics movement. This movement began because people started to challenge unfair beliefs about people with intellectual disabilities.

In the early 1960s, Dr. Frank Hayden, a Canadian sport scientist at the University of Toronto, studied students at Beverley School in Toronto. At that time, many people believed children with intellectual disabilities could not be successful in physical activity or sports.

Dr. Hayden’s research showed something very important. He found that low fitness was not mainly caused by disability itself. It was caused by a lack of chances to play, train, and participate.

His research influenced Eunice Kennedy Shriver, who helped organize the first Special Olympics event in Chicago in 1968. Canada was represented there by students from Beverley School. Later, Harry “Red” Foster helped bring Special Olympics to Canada, and the first Canadian Special Olympics event was held in Toronto in 1969.

Special Olympics BC began in 1980 with two weekends of games and about 500 participants. Since then, it has grown into year-round programs across British Columbia.

Special Olympics BC connects strongly to the social model of disability.

The social model says that disability is not only about a person’s diagnosis or impairment. Disability is also created by barriers in society. These barriers can include low expectations, inaccessible activities, negative attitudes, lack of support, and exclusion.

Special Olympics BC helps remove these barriers. It gives children and adults with intellectual disabilities real opportunities to train, compete, make friends, and be recognized for their strengths.

This contributes to disability rights because rights are not only about laws. Rights are also about real access, participation, dignity, and respect.

For example, if a person is never invited to join a sport, the barrier is not only inside the person. The barrier is also in the environment. But when the rules, coaching, and support are changed, that person can participate and succeed.

Special Olympics BC changes public attitudes. It helps people move from pity to respect, and from exclusion to inclusion.

First, we can see the idea moving from just present, to participating, and finally to belonging.

Being just present means the student is in the same classroom or gym, but may not really be involved. They may only watch, wait, or feel left out.

The next step is participating. This means the student is doing the activity, making choices, practising skills, and joining with others.

The final goal is belonging. Belonging means the student feels accepted, valued, and part of the group.

Special Olympics BC connects to inclusion in several ways.

First, there are youth programs. Programs like Active Start and FUNdamentals help children develop movement skills, sport skills, social skills, and confidence.

Second, there is Unified Sports. In Unified Sports, students with and without intellectual disabilities train and compete on the same team.

This is very important because it changes “helping” into teamwork. Students are not separated into “helpers” and “students who need help.” Instead, everyone has a role, and everyone participates as teammates.

Third, Special Olympics BC also gives ideas for inclusive gym activities. For example, a teacher or EA can use a bigger ball, slow down the pace, create partner roles, give visual instructions, and focus on teamwork goals.

So, real inclusion is not only about being in the same place. Real inclusion means students participate, build friendships, and feel that they belong.

First, an EA can break steps down. SOBC has ready-made lesson plans that already divide sport skills into short, clear steps. This helps students understand one part at a time.

Second, an EA can use visuals. The EA can use demonstrations, pictures, gestures, and activity guides from SOBC FUNdamentals to help students see what they need to do.

Third, an EA can model skills. In Unified Sports, everyone should have a meaningful role. The EA can model how to take turns, join a team, follow rules, and participate respectfully.

Fourth, an EA can build peer support. SOBC Unified Sports pairs students with and without intellectual disabilities as teammates. They train and compete together, just like any other school team.

Fifth, an EA can adapt activities. SOBC uses a divisioning approach. This means students can be grouped by ability, so every student can compete and participate at a fair level.

There are also specific SOBC resources for EAs. For younger children, Active Start, for ages 2 to 6, and FUNdamentals, for ages 7 to 11, support motor skills and sport skills. These programs can be used in communities and schools.

For older students, Unified Sports, for ages 12 to 18, provides lesson plans for sports such as 3-on-3 basketball, soccer, and floorball. Students practise and compete together as part of a team.

To access these resources, EAs can visit the Special Olympics BC website, especially the Youth Programs and School Sport pages. They can also ask the school or district about registering as a Unified Champion School or joining a school sport program.

For example, a student with a disability may feel nervous in gym class. The EA can download a FUNdamentals lesson plan, preview the rules, break down the skill, model the first turn, and then pair the student with a peer.

This shows that inclusion is active. An excellent EA does not just tell students they are welcome. An excellent EA plans, supports, adapts, and creates real chances for students to participate.

Suggested time: about 2 minutes
Listen to model audio
Ready to record

To summarize, Special Olympics BC began from a simple but powerful idea: people with intellectual disabilities need opportunity, support, and respect.

The movement grew from Dr. Frank Hayden’s research in Toronto, the first Special Olympics event in 1968, the first Canadian event in 1969, and the beginning of Special Olympics BC in 1980.

Special Olympics BC contributes to rights by removing barriers and changing public attitudes. It helps children and adults with intellectual disabilities participate in sports, build friendships, and be seen for their abilities.

It also connects strongly to schools because inclusion means real participation, not just being present.

Short Class Activity

Now we have a short class activity. We will read three examples. Please decide if each one shows a barrier, inclusion, or segregation.

Example one: A student watches gym class from the side because the game is too fast.

This is a barrier.

Example two: The teacher changes the rules so every student has a role on the team.

This is inclusion.

Example three: Students with disabilities play only in a separate room and never join classmates.

This is segregation, not full inclusion.

Opportunity + Support = Inclusion
