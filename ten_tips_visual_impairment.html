Inclusive Communication

Ten Tips for Communicating with Someone Who Has a Visual Impairment

Simple, respectful practices that can make everyday communication more natural, clear, and inclusive.

Introduction

Henshaws supports people with sight loss and a range of other disabilities to live independently and go beyond expectations.

Welcome to Henshaws’ Tips, Tricks and Tech. Normally, these videos offer advice to people with sight loss. This guide is slightly different: it gives ten useful tips to sighted people about communicating with someone who has a visual impairment.

1

Identify yourself

When entering a room, say hello and introduce yourself by name. When you leave, say goodbye so the person knows that you are no longer there.

2

Ask about the person’s level of vision

Do not assume how much a person can or cannot see. Ask what kind of support they need and how they would prefer you to help.

3

Explain who is present

In a group situation, make it clear how many people are in the group and identify everyone. Tell the person when someone enters or leaves.

4

Be descriptive

Do not simply say, “It’s over here,” or “It’s over there.” Give clear and specific directions, such as, “The chair is two steps in front of you,” or “Your cup is on your right.”

5

Ask before offering support

Do not automatically take hold of or guide someone. Ask whether they would like help first. Some people may want support, while others may not.

6

Use everyday language

There is no need to avoid phrases such as “See you later” or “Did you watch that movie?” People with visual impairments use these expressions too. Speak naturally and normally.

7

Speak directly to the person

Do not speak to a companion, support worker, or another person on their behalf. Address the person with the visual impairment directly.

8

Relax

Do not become overly nervous or self-conscious. Relax and communicate as you normally would with anyone else.

9

Use natural body language

Continue using normal gestures, facial expressions, and eye contact. Even if the person cannot see all your movements, natural body language helps your voice and communication sound more comfortable and genuine.

10

Do not talk to or distract a guide dog

Speak directly to the guide-dog owner, not to the dog. A guide dog is working and should not be distracted, touched, or given instructions without the owner’s permission.

These simple tips can help make communication more respectful, natural, and inclusive for people with visual impairments.
Accessible communication begins with respect, clarity, and asking before helping.
