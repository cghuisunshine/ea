Ruby Support Profile

The image summarizes how Ruby participates in different parts of the school day, where she is independent, and where adult, peer, home, or specialist support helps.

Overall meaning

Ruby can take part in classroom, social, and physical activities when expectations are clear and support is adjusted to the activity. The notes show a mix of independence, adapted tools, peer connection, one-on-one adult help, and goals that build confidence.

Classroom Learning

Reading

  • Ruby reads independently.
  • She uses the same reading bin as others.
  • She works at her own level.

This suggests inclusion with individualized expectations: Ruby joins the same routine while using materials that match her current reading level.

Instruction

  • Support can come from the teacher or an Education Assistant.
  • One-on-one instruction is useful when needed.

The support is not only about completing tasks. It also helps Ruby understand directions, stay engaged, and access the same lesson as her classmates.

Math

  • During lessons, Ruby works beside the teacher.
  • For math bins, she works with a helpful and friendly peer.

Math support combines adult proximity during direct teaching with peer support during practice. The peer relationship matters because the note names the peer as helpful and friendly.

Journal Share

  • Ruby benefits from teacher support.
  • Repeating information helps.
  • She may need help as the activity continues.

For speaking or sharing tasks, Ruby may need repeated prompts and flexible help so she can participate without being rushed.

Social Participation

Library

  • Ruby sits with peers.
  • She raises her hand.
  • Her answers are accepted.

The library notes show positive participation: Ruby joins peers, follows the group routine, and is given space for her responses to count.

Recess

  • The note says "H+S". The exact abbreviation should be confirmed locally.
  • Different rules may apply at recess.

Recess may need explicit expectations and supervision because outdoor or unstructured time often has different social and safety rules from classroom time.

Lunch

  • Ruby ate with a friend.
  • A handshake is noted as part of the interaction.

These details show social connection. The support goal is likely to keep these successful peer interactions happening naturally.

Hall

  • High fives are listed for the hallway.

A simple high five can be a quick positive connection during transitions and may help Ruby feel recognized and successful.

Adapted Activities

Art

  • Ruby uses adapted scissors.

This is a tool adaptation. It lets Ruby participate in the same art task with a tool that better fits her motor or access needs.

P.E.

  • Some activities are adapted.
  • A dance break is helpful.

Physical Education may need modified activities, but the note also points to movement as a positive support strategy.

Extra Support

S/P

  • The note says Ruby gets extra support at home.
  • The abbreviation "S/P" should be confirmed before using it in formal notes.

The key idea is that support is shared between school and home, not limited to one setting.

Extra support sometimes

  • An Education Assistant may help.
  • A specialist teacher may help.

The word "sometimes" matters. Ruby does not need every support all the time, but the team should know which supports to bring in when a task or situation calls for them.

Pride

Language goal: "not but" to "and"

The note shows a communication goal of shifting from "not but" language to "and" language. In practice, this means framing feedback or self-talk in a more connected way. For example, instead of treating difficulty and success as opposites, Ruby can learn to say that something is hard and she can keep trying.
