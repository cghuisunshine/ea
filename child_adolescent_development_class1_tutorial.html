Lesson 1

Start with the person supporting the child

The class begins with a central idea: an Educational Assistant’s practice is shaped by who the EA is. Self-reflection helps us notice the beliefs, values, biases and habits that influence our decisions.

Slides 4–15
Key principle: You cannot thoughtfully assess, challenge or improve your practice without examining the lens through which you interpret children.

Beliefs

Ideas you take to be true, such as “children need authority” or “children are capable.”

Values

What you consider important, such as compliance, curiosity, independence or cooperation.

Temperament

Your habitual emotional style, while remembering that people respond differently across situations.

Culture

Language, food, religion, dress, traditions, rituals and shared ways of interpreting the world.

Interpersonal skills

Skills such as active listening, reading body language, giving feedback and repairing misunderstandings.

Experiences

Past events that may shape your responses consciously or unconsciously.

Lesson 2

What is child development?

Child development is the study of predictable patterns of constancy and change from conception through adolescence. Theories work like lenses: each highlights some features while leaving others less visible.

Slides 16–19

How knowledge develops

  1. Researchers ask questions based on current theories.
  2. They gather and organize evidence.
  3. Evidence supports, revises or replaces explanations.
  4. Biological and environmental influences are considered together.

Why EAs study development

  • Set appropriate expectations.
  • Notice when skills may not be developing as expected.
  • Contribute to appropriate support plans.
  • Support families respectfully.
  • Guide children from their current level, not from an imagined “average” child.
EA application: Developmental knowledge should increase understanding, not become a label. A developmental range is a guide for observation and support—not a reason to assume every child will develop in the same way or at the same speed.
Lesson 3

Three connected domains of development

The domains are separated for study, but they operate together in real life. A change in one domain often affects the other two.

Slides 20–24

🧠 Cognitive

Memory, problem solving, thinking, reasoning and language development.

Example: A child learns to plan the steps needed to complete a science task.

🤝 Psychosocial

Self-concept, personality, emotions and relationships with other people.

Example: A student begins to see themself as a capable group member.

🏃 Biosocial

Genes, environment, physical growth, movement, strength, sensory capacity, health and nutrition.

Example: Improved hand strength supports handwriting and participation.

Try classifying these examples

Primarily cognitive (language), with psychosocial effects because language supports relationships.
All three domains: biosocial skill, cognitive learning and psychosocial confidence/belonging.
All three domains: a biosocial condition influences cognitive attention and psychosocial regulation.
Lesson 4

Five periods of development

Periods are based more on functional changes than exact birthdays. Ages are approximate and development is variable.

Slides 25–26
PrenatalConception to birth

Rapid biological development; the prenatal environment is the first context.

Infancy & toddlerhoodBirth to 2 years

Motor, sensory, attachment and early communication growth.

Early childhood2 to 6 years

Language, play, self-regulation and expanding independence.

Middle childhood6 to 11 years

School skills, peer relationships, logical thinking and competence.

Adolescence11 to 18+ years

Puberty, identity, abstract thought, autonomy and changing social roles.

Avoid rigid expectations: A child may be advanced in one domain and need substantial support in another. Development can be uneven.
Lesson 5

Major developmental debates and contexts

Slides 28–40

Historical views of children

Miniature adults

A medieval view that did not recognize childhood as a distinct developmental period.

Original sin

A Puritan view that infants were born innately bad and required strict control.

Tabula rasa

John Locke: the child as a “blank slate” shaped largely by experience.

Innate goodness

Jean-Jacques Rousseau: children are born good and should be allowed to develop naturally.

Three contemporary questions

QuestionView AView BBalanced interpretation
Continuous or discontinuous?Continuous: gradual, cumulative growth.Discontinuous: stage-like shifts to new ways of thinking or responding.Some abilities grow gradually; others show noticeable transitions.
One course or many?A common developmental path.Different paths shaped by context and culture.Shared human patterns exist, but their timing and expression vary.
Nature or nurture?Genes, biology and maturation.Education, parenting, environment and policy.Development usually reflects continuous interaction between both.

Contexts that shape development

Pregnancy and healthFamily structureParentingCulture and communityPeersEthnicity and raceSocial class / SESHistorical generation

Authoritative parenting

High warmth and acceptance, clear control, and appropriately high expectations.

Authoritarian parenting

Low warmth and high control/demand. Do not confuse authoritarian with authoritative.

Critical caution: Our own historical and cultural context can make one way of raising children feel like the only “right” way. Professional practice requires curiosity, humility and attention to power.
Lesson 6

Five characteristics of development

Slides 41–47

Multidirectional

Change includes gains, losses, pauses and unexpected turns.

Multicontextual

Development is embedded in historical, economic, family and cultural contexts.

Multicultural

Children grow within groups that share values, customs and traditions.

Multidisciplinary

Biology, education, psychology, sociology, anthropology and medicine all contribute.

Plasticity

Change remains possible, but it builds on previous development and biological constraints.

EA application: Plasticity supports a growth-oriented approach. A student’s current performance is not a fixed limit. At the same time, effective support begins with the child’s real history, strengths and needs.
Lesson 7

Research literacy: correlation is not causation

Slides 49–53

Correlational research

Describes the strength of a relationship between variables.

Can show: two things vary together.

Cannot by itself show: that one thing caused the other.

Experimental research

Tests causation by manipulating an independent variable while controlling other factors.

Independent variable: the factor changed.

Dependent variable: the outcome measured.

What to look for in a study

Example: If screen time and sleep difficulty are correlated, several explanations remain possible: screen use may affect sleep, poor sleepers may use screens more, or a third factor may influence both.
Lesson 8

Six influential theories as classroom lenses

A theory is not a complete picture of a child. It is a lens that helps us ask useful questions and select supports.

Slides 54–76
Piaget
Cognitive

Children actively construct systems of thinking

Children organize, classify and manipulate information to make sense of experience. Assimilation fits new information into an existing idea; accommodation changes the idea. Equilibration is the process of restoring cognitive balance.

StageApproximate ageMain pattern
SensorimotorBirth–2Learning through senses and physical action.
Preoperational2–7Words, images, drawings and symbolic play.
Concrete operational7–11Logical reasoning with concrete examples.
Formal operational11+ through adulthoodMore abstract, idealistic and logical reasoning.
EA lens: Ask what the student already understands. Use concrete materials, demonstrations and examples before expecting abstract reasoning.
Erikson
Psychosocial

Development unfolds through social crises or turning points

Each stage presents a developmental task. A crisis is not a catastrophe; it is a point of vulnerability and possibility.

Childhood and adolescence
  1. Trust / mistrust
  2. Autonomy / shame and doubt
  3. Initiative / guilt
  4. Industry / inferiority
  5. Identity / role confusion
Adulthood
  1. Intimacy / isolation
  2. Generativity / stagnation
  3. Integrity / despair
EA lens: Create opportunities for autonomy, initiative, competence and identity without using shame or over-helping.
Skinner
Behaviourism

Consequences influence observable behaviour

In operant conditioning, behaviours followed by reinforcing consequences become more likely, while behaviours followed by punishing consequences become less likely. Positive means adding something; negative means removing something.

Add somethingRemove something
Increase behaviourPositive reinforcement: add a desired consequence.Negative reinforcement: remove an unpleasant condition.
Decrease behaviourPositive punishment: add an unpleasant consequence.Negative punishment: remove a desired item or privilege.
EA lens: Identify the function and consequence of behaviour. Reinforce a specific replacement behaviour instead of relying only on punishment.
Bandura
Social Learning

People learn by observing models

Behaviour, environment and personal cognition influence one another. Through observational learning, children can acquire behaviours, thoughts and feelings by watching what others do. The Bobo doll experiment is the course example.

EA lens: Model the behaviour you want to see, narrate your strategy, and choose peer models carefully. Adults are always teaching through their own conduct.
Vygotsky
Sociocultural

Learning is social, cultural and collaborative

Culture and social interaction guide development. Knowledge is situated and collaborative, and interaction with more skilled adults or peers can advance cognitive development.

Zone of proximal development (ZPD): the gap between what a learner can do independently and what they can achieve with guidance.

Scaffolding: temporary, adjusted support that enables success and is gradually reduced as competence increases.

EA lens: Give the least intrusive support that makes success possible, then fade it. The goal is increased independence, not permanent adult assistance.
Bronfenbrenner
Ecological

Development occurs within interacting environmental systems

SystemMeaningSchool example
MicrosystemSettings the child directly experiences.Family, school, peers and neighbourhood.
MesosystemConnections between microsystems.Family–school communication.
ExosystemSettings that affect the child indirectly.A parent’s work schedule or district policy.
MacrosystemWider culture, values, laws and beliefs.Ideas about disability, education and inclusion.
ChronosystemTime, transitions and historical events.Moving schools, family change or a major social event.
EA lens: Do not locate every difficulty “inside” the student. Ask what in the task, relationships, environment, schedule, policy or history may be affecting participation.
Using theories together: A student struggling with group work may need concrete instruction (Piaget), a stronger sense of competence (Erikson), reinforcement for a replacement skill (Skinner), a model to observe (Bandura), scaffolded peer support (Vygotsky), and changes across classroom or family systems (Bronfenbrenner).

Key-term glossary

Accommodation: changing an existing cognitive framework to fit new information.
Assimilation: fitting new information into an existing cognitive framework.
Biosocial development: physical, biological, sensory and health-related change.
Causation: evidence that one factor produces change in another.
Cognitive development: change in thinking, memory, reasoning, problem solving and language.
Correlation: a measured relationship between variables that does not by itself establish cause.
Dependent variable: the measured outcome in an experiment.
Equilibration: Piaget’s process of restoring cognitive balance through assimilation or accommodation.
Independent variable: the factor manipulated in an experiment.
Plasticity: the capacity for developmental change within biological and historical constraints.
Psychosocial development: change in self-concept, emotions, personality and relationships.
Reliability: consistency of a measure or finding, including replication.
Scaffolding: temporary, adjusted support that is faded as a learner gains competence.
Validity: how well a study or measure captures what it claims to examine.
ZPD: the range between independent performance and performance possible with guidance.
