2. Inclusion Concepts Tree

  • Inclusion
    • Belonging — students feel welcomed and valued
    • Equity — students receive what they need to access learning
    • Accessibility — barriers are removed before they block participation
    • Meaningful participation — students join classroom life at their level
    • Differentiation — different ways to learn and show understanding
    • Collaboration — teacher, EA, family, specialists, and student voice work together
    • Respect for diversity — ability, culture, language, identity, and family background are respected
Simple definition: Inclusion means all students have equitable access to learning and are supported to participate meaningfully with others, not just placed in the same room.

3. Four Key Concepts

Not included Exclusion

Picture: the student is not at the table.

School example: Eric is asked to stay home from the beach picnic because of wheelchair access.

Separated Segregation

School example: Margaret goes to a separate mixed-grade room for students with learning differences.

Placed together Integration

Picture: students are at the same table, but separated.

Belongs Inclusion

Picture: students are at the same table and mixed together.

School example: the activity is adapted so the student participates with peers.

Quick memory trick: Exclusion = outside the table. Segregation = different tables. Integration = same table, but separated. Inclusion = same table, mixed.

4. Integration vs Inclusion

IntegrationInclusion
The student joins the regular setting.The setting is designed so the student can belong and participate.
The student may need to adjust to the existing system.The adults adjust instruction, materials, expectations, and supports.
Can become “breathing the same air” without real participation.Focuses on meaningful participation and interaction with peers.
Often adult-dependent or separate work.Teacher-led learning with EA support, peer connection, and appropriate goals.

Integration（融合/整合）：学生进入普通教室，但可能只是坐在那里，学习任务仍然分开。
Inclusion（包容）：教室、教学和支持方式会改变，让学生真正参与和归属。

5. Quick Check: Which Concept?

Scenario A: Margaret

Margaret is a Grade 3 student with Down Syndrome. Her brother goes to a Grade 3/4 classroom, but Margaret is walked down the hall to a mixed-grade room for students with learning differences.

Segregation. Margaret is in the same school, but she is separated from her age/grade peers in a different room.

Scenario B: Eric

The school is going to Kits Beach for a picnic. The administration asks Eric’s parents to keep him home because it will be difficult to use his wheelchair on the sand.

Exclusion. Eric is not given access to the school activity. Inclusion would require planning for accessibility.

Scenario C: “Breathing the same air”

A student is in the regular classroom, but works on a separate curriculum at the back of the room with an EA and has little interaction with classmates.

Integration, not inclusion. The student is placed in the room, but the activity is not meaningfully connected to the class.

6. What Inclusion Is and Is Not

Does not mean
  • Separate curriculum in the back of the classroom with an EA.
  • Students sitting passively while adults do the work.
  • Expecting every student to meet the exact same goals in the exact same way.
Means
  • Students work on the same classroom topic or activity when possible.
  • Students are actively involved at their own level of ability.
  • Teacher directs learning; EA supports access, participation, and independence.
  • Individualized goals may be used when needed.
Fair does not always mean everyone gets the same thing. Fair means everyone can access the learning.

7. Benefits of Inclusion

Inclusion can benefit all learners, including students with disabilities or diverse abilities and typically developing peers.

Social skills

Building relationships, peer interaction, respectful feedback.

Communication skills

Learning to express needs and understand others.

Academic skills

Learning through participation in class activities.

Coping skills

Managing routines, conflict, boundaries, and change.

EA connection: An excellent EA supports participation without taking over. The goal is access, independence, dignity, and peer connection.

8. Adaptations, Modifications, Accommodations

TermMeaningExample
AdaptationChanges to environment, materials, instruction, assessment, equipment, or how work is completed.Audio books, enlarged font, extra time, lower reading level, visual instructions.
ModificationChanges to the content, learning goals, or skills the student is expected to master.Instead of writing a full planet report, the student identifies planets and says their names.
AccommodationOften used similarly to adaptation; a support that increases access without changing the core learning goal.Wheelchair, eyeglasses, lined paper, adjustable seat, speech-to-text.

9. Inclusive Activity Examples

Penguin research

Class activity: Research penguins and make a poster about appearance, habitat, diet, and life cycle.

Inclusive adapted/modified option: Label penguin body parts and identify at least two foods penguins eat.

Silent reading

Class activity: Read silently for 10 minutes.

Inclusive option: Look at picture books quietly or use electronic books on an iPad.

Calendar circle time

Class activity: Participate in calendar routines.

Inclusive option: Tell the teacher the date/day, or press play to start the days-of-the-week song.

Harry Potter summary

Class activity: Read the book and write a summary.

Inclusive option: Make a 5-minute group movie, watch the movie, or sequence photos of scenes.

Your turn: Salmon booklet activity

Class activity: Make a booklet with 10 written journal entries from the perspective of a salmon, including events and challenges from egg to adult salmon.

Possible adapted/modified ideas:

  • Use picture cards to sequence the salmon life cycle.
  • Choose 3 important stages instead of 10 written entries.
  • Use sentence starters: “I am an egg. I need ___.”
  • Record an audio journal instead of writing.
  • Work with a peer to match dangers/challenges to each stage.

Think: What is the purpose of the activity? Life cycle knowledge? Perspective writing? Sequencing? Vocabulary? The support should match the purpose.

10. Student Voice: “Nothing About Us Without Us”

The phrase reminds educators that people with disabilities should be included in decisions that affect their lives. In schools, this means students should have a voice in their learning plans, supports, communication preferences, and goals.

Ask

How does the student prefer to receive help?

Listen

Notice communication, choices, discomfort, and strengths.

Include

Involve the student in goal-setting when possible.

EA reflection: Support should not be only adult control. It should remove barriers and protect dignity.

11. School Support Teams

School-Based Team

May include administrator, learning support teacher, classroom teacher, EA, counsellor, speech-language pathologist, and other school staff.

District Team

May include district administrators, district learning support teachers, school psychologists, behaviour analysts, DHH/vision teachers, and mental health liaisons.

Community/Health Supports

For example, Fraser Health CSRT may support students with physical disabilities, developmental disabilities, or complex medical needs.

Important: Parents/caregivers should be informed when a student is referred to the school-based team. A case manager may coordinate when the team is large.

12. Ministry Policy Review

Use these notes to study for the Ministry of Education Inclusive Education Policy Manual assignment.

1) Rationale of the policy

Inclusive education programs and services support students with disabilities or diverse abilities to have equitable access to learning and opportunities to pursue and achieve their educational goals.

School example: A class activity is adapted or modified so students can participate with peers instead of being removed from the activity.

2) Types of disabilities students may have

Examples include intellectual, physical, sensory, emotional, or behavioural disabilities or diverse abilities.

3) Alternatives to full-time placement in regular classrooms

Appropriate alternatives may include resource rooms, self-contained classes, community-based programs, or specialized settings.

4) What must school boards consult parents about?

Parents must have an opportunity to be consulted about the student’s IEP and placement.

5) Do all students with disabilities or diverse abilities need an IEP?

No. An IEP may not be required when the student needs little or no adaptations, the expected learning outcomes are not modified, and the student receives 25 or fewer hours of remedial instruction from someone other than the classroom teacher in a school year.

6) Are students only evaluated on grade/course learning standards?

False. In rare cases, students with significant cognitive disabilities or diverse abilities may be assessed only on individualized learning goals in their IEP.

15. One-Minute Summary

Inclusion is more than location. A student can be in the same room and still not be included. True inclusion means barriers are removed, the student has meaningful participation, and the classroom community values the student’s presence, voice, strengths, and learning goals.
